2022 Honda Civic: The Compact Car King Gets Even Better It’ll blow your mind.

Sedans may be losing ground in the sales race to crossovers, but the Honda Civic still dominates the small-car market. Honda sold 260,000 of them in 2020 despite both Covid-19’s disruptions and the basic car being half a decade old. For 2022, the Civic gets entirely redesigned for its eleventh generation, shedding the previous models’ odd boy-racer looks for a sleek and mature shape and a more user-friendly, stylish interior. With the Honda Fit now discontinued, the Civic is again the entry-level to Honda’s lineup for the first time since the mid-2000s. Drivers of older Civics may be surprised to learn that at 184 inches long, the car is now about the same size as a mid-1990s Accord. They’ll be happy to discover its roomier inside than that old midsize and that it retains all of the traditional Civic virtues. Even in base model form it’s fun to drive, comfy to be in, and value-packed. Honda knows that the Civic is, as always, popular with first time buyers and therefore puts a great deal of effort into hooking those customers on a lifetime of Hondas. The company is also conscious of just how good the Civic’s main rivals, primarily the Hyundai Elantra and Mazda3, have become in recent years.

2022 Volkswagen Golf GTI: Sports-Car Thrills On A Hatchback Budget See for yourself.

ans of the Volkswagen Golf—always a top-selling hatchback globally, but an underdog here—are free to shed a tear: After a nearly 50-year run, VW stopped selling the standard Golf in the U.S. However, the German automaker still sells the GTI, the Golf’s hot-hatch version, along with an even-hotter Golf R with all-wheel-drive. Both models are all-new, based on the eighth-generation Golf. And what a consolation prize: The GTI is a knee-wobbling delight to drive and still more roomy and practical than typical performance hatchbacks. It’s also a solid value, starting from $30,540, including the destination charge, compared to $33,255 for a Hyundai Veloster N. The Golf GTI is faster than ever, with up to 241 horsepower from a 2.0-liter turbo four-cylinder. The upshot is a 5.1-second sprint to 60 mph, managed via a standard six-speed manual transmission or a seven-speed, dual-clutch automated gearbox that would be worthy in a car double or triple the price. The smooth-revving engine is a German jewel shared with several Audi models. But speed isn’t the only story. The GTI feels almost limitless in its ability to dispatch curves effortlessly at a breathtaking pace. An adaptive suspension (standard on the top-tier Autobahn trim) with 15 driver-adjustable settings and standard electronic limited-slip differential help the GTI dig out of corners faster than a prison escapee. It never loses grip from its handsome, standard 18-inch wheels or optional 19’s. Powerful brakes are up to the fast-paced task, and sensitive steering imbues any driver with confidence.
